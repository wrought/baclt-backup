BACLT History



The Bay Area Community Land Trust (BACLT) began in September 2005 with a meeting of community activists interested in housing options for aging baby boomers.


We decided on the Limited Equity Housing Cooperative (LEHC) within a Community Land Trust (CLT) as our model.  The Articles of Incorporation were filed in August 2006. The Board convened in September and elected a board of directors. 


In January 2007, we held a public meeting of 65 people to present our vision. We gained 20 new members at the meeting and the following month.   BACLT filed its application to become a 501c3 nonprofit in October 2007, just before its 2nd Annual Membership meeting.


The primary challenge in implementing our model co-op was, of course, funding.  In 2008, the housing bubble burst and the stock market fell.  People who had felt comfortable about retirement were reluctant to commit funds to this project. Further, to be affordable to lower income households, there would have to be some form of public subsidy to lower the buy-in cost.  Most federal funding programs are difficult to access for a home ownership coop project, so we looked to potential local programs that could supplement the financing.  In January 2008, we received our formal determination as a nonprofit from the IRS.


We continued trying to develop a project. As the financial situation worsened, more properties came on the market.  But we began to understand that a nonprofit organization like BACLT needed to have greater organizational capacity to gain the confidence of lenders and public agencies, if we were going to get loans and grants to achieve our goals.  We therefore decided to suspend our search for a large, new construction project that would involve millions of dollars investment, and started looking at some smaller conversion projects.


In 2010, we worked on three potential projects. 


The first was a three unit apartment building in Berkeley that was offered by the owners as a donation to BACLT.  We made significant progress working with the residents, incorporating as a coop and training the residents. This project was finally donated to BACLT in 2013 and is in the process of renovation.


The second project was the donation of the land at the 9th Street Coop in Berkeley.  The coop members agreed to donate the land, and the mortgage holder agreed to allow the transaction without refinancing.  This project is on hold due to a potential substantial increase in property taxes that could occur after the transfer of property.  The County Assessor has indicated that such a reassessment would occur, and hiring an attorney to argue against such a reassessment will be expensive.  We are still considering our options. 


The third project is a 7 bedroom collective home, called Brigid House in West Berkeley.  It is owned by two of the residents, and the goal is to form a nonprofit coop to take ownership.  We have signed a Memorandum of Understanding with Brigid House to help them convert to a co-op.


We have developed a full set of legal documents for new coop projects.  These include a Ground Lease, Articles of Incorporation, Bylaws and Occupancy Agreement for a coop. 


We became aware of the strong need for a formal training program for newly forming coops, and for existing coops, as well. In December 2010, we submitted a CDBG grant to the City of Berkeley to help fund this training program for lower income coops in Berkeley.  With funds for three years from the city of Berkeley we have provided trainings for ten Berkeley co-ops on elections and board recruitment. 


We opened a modest office in November 2010 and Rick Lewis, a board member with 30 years of experience with co-ops and land trusts, agreed to serve as the Interim Executive Director, on January 1, 2011.  He is now our Executive Director. 


As part of building support for housing co-ops, in 2012 BACLT convened the East Bay Co-op Coalition of more than 30 resident-controlled co-ops and collectives, which meets quarterly.


Our board includes a lawyer with extensive experience in co-ops and collective housing situations of all kinds, a past officer of the Berkeley Rent Board, residents of Berkeley co-ops, and other supporters of limited equity co-op housing.
